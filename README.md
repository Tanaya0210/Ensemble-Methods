# Ensemble-Methods
A Methodological Study of Ensemble Learning: Bagging and Boosting for Heart Disease Classification

The study aims to highlight how the structural features of decision-tree-based models impact predictive performance through their effect on bias-variance tradeoffs. To accomplish this, exploratory data analysis was performed to clean the dataset, followed by PCA for dimensionality reduction to maximize modelling efficiency. The baseline interpretability of a single decision tree was then established, which was further enhanced using ensemble approaches (bagging and gradient boosting) to reduce bias and variance in the predictions. Tuning of hyperparameters was undertaken for the optimal performance of each model, and four metrics (accuracy, ROC-AUC, cross-entropy loss, and F1-Score) were used to evaluate the results obtained. The results supported the theoretical view of bias and variance behavior and showed that although the decision tree model was interpretable, its predictive capabilities and level of stability were limited. The bagging method was applied to increase the stability of the model and increase accuracy because of the reduction of variance, while gradient boosting produced the highest level of performance. Ultimately, the results showed that the use of ensemble learning can considerably improve the predictive accuracy of decision trees beyond that of decision trees by themselves.
